How Peltier Modules Work

Peltier modules (TECs) are solid-state heat pumps that convert electrical energy into a temperature gradient using the Peltier effect. They can also work in reverse, generating electricity from temperature differences via the Seebeck effect.

Generating Electricity from Heat Difference

When a temperature difference is maintained across a Peltier module, charge carriers (electrons in N-type and holes in P-type semiconductors) diffuse from the hot side to the cold side, creating an electrical current.

V = α × ΔT

Where:

  • V = Voltage generated (Volts)
  • α = Seebeck coefficient (V/K)
  • ΔT = Temperature difference (K)

The efficiency depends on the figure of merit (ZT) which incorporates the Seebeck coefficient, electrical conductivity, and thermal conductivity.

Active Cooling with Electricity

When electrical current is applied to the Peltier module, charge carriers absorb thermal energy at one junction and release it at the other, creating a temperature difference.

Q = (α × I × Tc) - (½ × I² × R) - (K × ΔT)

Where:

  • Q = Heat pumped (Watts)
  • α = Seebeck coefficient (V/K)
  • I = Current (Amperes)
  • Tc = Cold side temperature (K)
  • R = Electrical resistance (Ω)
  • K = Thermal conductance (W/K)
  • ΔT = Temperature difference (K)

The coefficient of performance (COP) represents cooling efficiency: COP = Qc / Pin

Application in RSPG Technology

In the Radiative Sky Power Generator, Peltier modules are strategically used to harness both the Seebeck and Peltier effects for 24/7 energy generation.

Night-Time Operation

Using radiative cooling, the aluminum plate becomes colder than the ambient air, creating a temperature difference across the Peltier modules that generates electricity via the Seebeck effect.

Day-Time Operation

The solar panel provides electricity to actively cool one side of the Peltier modules using the Peltier effect, while the other side is heated by ambient temperature, maintaining the temperature gradient.

Efficiency Optimization

Our proprietary design maximizes the temperature difference and minimizes thermal losses, achieving higher efficiency than standard Peltier applications.
